About

Why Cloudforce exists

A blog written by a practitioner, for practitioners — because the Salesforce ecosystem deserves better technical writing.

Cloudforce started out of frustration. Not with Salesforce — with the writing about Salesforce. Most technical content in the ecosystem falls into one of two categories: marketing material dressed up as a blog post, or documentation that answers what but never why.

What I wanted — what I kept looking for and not finding — was writing that felt like sitting across from a senior colleague. Someone who would tell you the thing that took them three years to learn. Someone who would say "this is how it works in theory, but here's what actually happens in production."

That's what Cloudforce is trying to be.

What we cover

Everything on the Salesforce platform that a technical professional might need to understand deeply. That means Apex and LWC for developers, Flow and security models for admins, and integration architecture and data modelling for architects. Certifications, when we can say something useful that isn't already on the Trailhead.

We don't cover Salesforce releases for their own sake. When a new feature ships, we'll write about it only if we have something meaningful to say about how to use it well, when not to use it, or what it changes about existing patterns.

How articles are written

Every article starts with a question I've actually been asked, or a problem I've actually encountered. I write a first draft, then try to break it — running the code samples, stress-testing the claims, asking "is this actually true, or does it just sound true?"

Tutorials are meant to be actionable. You should be able to read one, close the tab, and do the thing. Deep Dives are meant to change how you think about a problem — they're longer and more discursive, and they're comfortable sitting with nuance.

Get in touch

If you've spotted an error, have a topic suggestion, or want to discuss something from an article, I'd genuinely like to hear from you. You can reach me on LinkedIn or on the Salesforce Trailblazer Community.

If you're a practitioner with something to say and want to contribute an article, see the contribution guidelines below.

Contributing

Cloudforce publishes contributions from practising Salesforce professionals. If you have real-world experience on a topic and can write clearly, we'd like to hear from you. The bar is high — articles need to be technically accurate, honest about tradeoffs, and useful to someone doing the work — but there's no word count requirement or traffic target for contributors.

Reach out with a brief pitch (one or two sentences) before writing anything. If it's a good fit, we'll work on it together.
